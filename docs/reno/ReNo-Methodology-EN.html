00 — About this document

What this whitepaper is

This paper defines the ReNo instrument: its theoretical foundations, its five measurement axes, its scoring and coding system, the shadow/growth-edge it derives, its profile architecture, and the empirical roadmap required to validate it.

It is a methodology document, not marketing material. It describes what ReNo measures and why, in terms a psychometrician or research collaborator would expect. The consumer-facing translation of these constructs — the warm, parent-facing language, the animal totems, the campaign copy — is deliberately kept in a separate layer and is out of scope here.

Honest status

ReNo is at present a theoretically specified but empirically unvalidated instrument. The constructs, axes, and scoring logic are defined here; reliability, factor structure, and population norms are roadmap items, not accomplished facts. Nothing here should be presented to customers or investors as a validated psychometric result until the development study in Section 10 is complete.

01 — Purpose & scope

One engine, two products

ReNo measures the stable structure of an individual's personality, tracks how it consolidates over time, and translates that structure into guidance about fit — educational, vocational, and developmental.

The instrument powers two products that share one engine but must never be conflated.

PsyID — adult

Self-report · English · 15–45

For people figuring out their lives — career fit, identity, decisions at a crossroads. No upper age limit; older adults are welcome, but it is built for those actively working out direction. Delivered as tiered reports.

PsyID Youth — children

Parent-observed · Russian · 8–14

A "psychological passport" the parent completes, yielding developmental guidance and profession directions for the child.

Why the youth floor is 8, not 6

Before roughly age 7–8 a child's personality is still highly malleable and its structure is not yet reliably measurable — early-childhood temperament is real but under-differentiated, and self- or parent-report at that age carries too much noise. The working theory is that as the brain matures, a measurable, increasingly stable core emerges. ReNo therefore begins at 8, treats the 8–14 window as a consolidating profile rather than a fixed one (Section 11), and at 15 hands off to the adult self-report instrument — a clean transition from parent-observed to self-observed.

The passport is longitudinal by design

Both products produce a passport — not a one-off result but a dated record the user builds up over repeated tests, so they can see their own personality history across years. This is a core feature, not an add-on: it is what turns a quiz into a developmental record, and it is the same mechanism that makes the stability study (Section 11) possible.

ReNo carries a dual mandate. Commercially, it produces reports and a passport people will pay to maintain. Scientifically, it is designed from day one to collect the item-level, timestamped, repeatable data needed to test a genuine question about personality stability and development.

02 — Theoretical foundations

Standing on several shoulders

ReNo is not a single-theory instrument. It draws several established traditions into one architecture, using McAdams' model of personality as the integrating spine.

On attribution & IP

Building a new instrument inspired by these thinkers is standard, legitimate scholarship, and naming them is the correct move. Scientific ideas and theories are not copyrightable — introversion, the RIASEC types, the three-level model are ideas, free to build on — and citation is what distinguishes theory-building from plagiarism. Two lines not to cross: (1) do not reproduce others' copyrighted expression — their exact descriptions, item wording, tables (ReNo paraphrases and rebuilds throughout); (2) do not use trademarks in branding — MBTI®, Myers-Briggs®, the branded 16-type names, the Self-Directed Search® — or imply their validation transfers to ReNo. The renaming in Section 4 exists precisely to stay clear of this line. General information, not legal advice; a short IP review before commercial launch is advisable.

A deliberate gap. ReNo's trait layer overlaps the Big Five, but ReNo is not a Big Five instrument. The Big Five is the external yardstick against which ReNo's axes will be validated (§12), not a component of the model.

03 — The three-level architecture

What each part is allowed to claim

Every ReNo profile is organized on McAdams' three levels. This governs what each part of a profile is permitted to claim.

LevelWhat it isReNo componentProduct tier
Level 1
Dispositional traits		Broad, decontextualized, relatively stable tendencies. "The psychology of the stranger."The five measured axes.Tier 1 — "Who am I?"
Level 2
Characteristic adaptations		Goals, motives, interests, coping — contextualized in role and time.RIASEC mapping; SDT needs; profession directions; the growth edge.Tier 2 — "Where do I fit?"

The rule this enforces

A measurement claim (Level 1) and a story (Level 3) are different kinds of statement. The axis scores may be defended as data; the archetype is explicitly a narrative and is never presented as a measured result. Keeping the levels distinct is what keeps ReNo honest as it scales from a quiz into a research instrument.

Naming discipline

Axes are named by the dimension, not a pole, so a 0–100 score reads as a position on a spectrum. Pole letters are language-neutral and visually distinct (O / W avoid "I reads as one" and the E-collision; D = orDered). The system abandons all MBTI and socionics vocabulary — both to avoid others' IP and to resolve a collision: Jung uses "rational" to mean judging, which the old labels contradicted.

06 — Construct hygiene

What was separated

The original rubric measured behaviour richly but let distinct constructs bleed across axes. An axis carrying two constructs cannot factor cleanly, and cross-loading items are the main reason scores drift year to year for reasons that are not real change. v1.1 performs a deliberate separation.

Descriptor in the old rubricWas attached toAction in v1.1
"Stays composed while others lose control"Axis 3 — Decision Basis→ Move to Axis 5. Stress tolerance, not decision logic.
"Emotional stability"Axis 4 — Structure→ Move to Axis 5. Never a structure construct.
"Mood swings drive productivity"Axis 4 — Structure→ Move to Axis 5. Emotional tempo.
Risk propensityAxis 4 — Structure→ Drop as a direct measure. A blend of low-Conscientiousness, low-Neuroticism, high-Extraversion — cross-loads on three axes. Let it emerge, don't measure it.
Conservative vs innovatorAxis 4 — Structure→ Fold into Axis 2. Openness-to-novelty is the same signal as Concrete↔Abstract.

After this pass, Axis 4 measures only order-vs-spontaneity and starter-vs-finisher — clean Conscientiousness — and the emotional content now lives, cleanly, on Axis 5. This is the version that survives factor analysis and gives a trustworthy longitudinal read.

07 — Type-naming architecture

The code is the engine; the name is the dashboard

The user never remembers five axes. They remember one name. The two products use different naming skins over the same code spine.

Youth — animal totems

Job: reassurance + pride

A parent remembers "my daughter is a Fox." The animal is the System 1 hook; the methodology sits below the fold for the involved parent.

Adult — archetype roles

Job: self-understanding + decisions

"The Steady Builder," "The Quick Improviser." A named role reads as insight and carries authority; an adult told they're an "Owl" feels infantilized.

Three rules that hold the system together

  • Shared invisible spine. The five-axis code is identical across both products — what makes them one family, not two quizzes.
  • The name is a mapping, not a spelling. Do not force the axis letters to spell a word; a name is a label bolted onto a region of the code space, as "The Architect" is bolted onto a coordinate.
  • Name off the dominant, high-band axis. A near-balanced profile (band 0–1) gets a distinct "adaptable / versatile" name rather than being jammed into a strong pole.
The cross-sell bridge

Each child animal maps to its adult archetype on the same axis-pole (child "Fox" → adult "Improviser"). This pairing is the literal mechanism of cross-selling: a parent already committed to the framework for their child converts more readily on themselves (Cialdini's commitment & consistency). Axis 5 is excluded from the headline name — no child is "The Reactive One" — and any animal set must be sense-checked for connotation in the target culture.

08 — The shadow / growth edge

The core holds; the shadow grows

Every profile has a front and a back. The dominant preferences are the front — the persona a person leads with. The shadow is the back: the underdeveloped opposite poles, and especially the least-developed axis, where a person operates less consciously. ReNo surfaces this as the growth edge.

Growth edge · derived, not measured
A Level 2/3 interpretation — no code slot

The shadow is computed from the existing five axes — the opposite pole of each dominant preference, weighted toward the least-developed axis (the one closest to balanced, or lowest-band among the leading functions). It gets no code slot and no confidence band of its own.

Why it is the reason to re-test

Jung's claim is not that a person never changes — it is that the core preferences are stable while the shadow / inferior functions develop over the lifespan (individuation; Lawrence's "type development"). The structure holds; the shadow grows. That resolves the apparent paradox of a longitudinal product: if personality were fully fixed, why re-test? Because the passport tracks two things at once — the stable core (which should hold) and the developing growth edge (which should move). The re-test is not looking for the core to change; it is watching the shadow mature.

Framing rule — this one is sensitive

For adults, "shadow / untapped potential / where you get in your own way" is a compelling hook. For a child's profile shown to a parent, "shadow" and "not living up to potential" must never read as a deficit or a dark verdict on the child. The parent-facing frame is strengths-based and developmental — "growth edge," "emerging strengths," "where your child will benefit from support" — grounded in SDT, never a label. Same construct, life-stage-appropriate language, exactly as with Axis 5.

The least-developed axis points the shadow toward developing outward expression: speaking up, initiating. Framed for a parent as "where a little encouragement pays off," not a weakness.

What the three levels contain

  • Level 1 — measured. The five axes as spectrum positions, each with a confidence band, not a false-precision point ("71, likely 64–78"). Visualized as a radar. The band protects the retest promise.
  • Level 2 — interpretive. The code translated into patterns: RIASEC direction and profession fit, SDT motivational profile, and the growth edge (§8).
  • Level 3 — narrative. The archetype/totem and a short life-story framing — the part a person screenshots. Explicitly a story.

The passport

Each test writes a dated entry to the user's passport. Over years, the passport shows two trend-lines: the stable core (dominant axes, expected to hold within their confidence bands) and the growth edge (expected to develop). For the youth product it states plainly that it is based on parent observation, which sets up the annual re-test naturally.

10 — Measurement development

The DeVellis path

To move ReNo from "specified" to "validated," it follows the standard scale-development sequence (DeVellis, Scale Development).

  1. Define each construct. Done — §4, with explicit "not-this" boundaries.
  2. Generate a broad item pool. ~15–20 candidate items per axis, deliberately redundant, so weak items drop and a reliable ~10-item scale remains.
  3. Fix the response format. Likert agreement, continuous scoring behind the bands.
  4. Expert review for content validity — the role for the credentialed collaborator.
  5. Include validation items (§12) so convergent validity is tested in the same sitting.
  6. Administer to a development sample. The launch itself is a viable first data-collection engine.
  7. Item & factor analysis — confirm five clean factors; drop cross-loading items.
  8. Optimize scale length — reliability vs brevity.

Item design rules

  • One construct per item — the point of §6.
  • Balanced reverse-scoring so acquiescence bias does not inflate a pole.
  • Behaviourally anchored — an observable action, never a trait word. Parents rate behaviour accurately but infer traits poorly.
  • Two phrasings — adult self-report, and parent-observer for the 8–14 youth version.

11 — The stability hypothesis

Does structure stabilize as the brain matures?

ReNo's guiding question. Users re-test over time and profiles are compared. This section states honestly what the literature supports and what the design must do to yield a real finding rather than an artifact.

What the evidence actually shows

  • A measurable core emerges in middle childhood. The empirical basis for the age-8 floor: below ~7–8 the Big Five structure is under-differentiated; by 8 it is crystallized enough to assess. The "stabilizes as the brain develops" theory is directionally supported.
  • Rank-order stability rises with age but is not yet high in 8–14. Consistency climbs through childhood and only plateaus near age 50 (Roberts & DelVecchio). The honest framing is "an increasingly stable core," not "fixed."
  • Mean-level maturation happens even in stable people (the maturity principle): normative drift toward more conscientiousness, agreeableness, emotional stability. Rank can hold while absolute scores move.
  • The core holds while the shadow develops (§8) — the developmental claim ReNo actually makes.
The design trap — and why continuous scoring is the fix

A categorical type re-tested annually manufactures instability: a person near a cut-point flips on trivial fluctuation. ReNo measures stability on the continuous 0–100 axes, never bands or type — which requires knowing test-retest reliability first, because without it true change cannot be separated from measurement error. That separation is the entire study.

Design requirements

  • Store and analyze raw continuous scores with item-level responses and timestamps, from launch.
  • Establish test-retest reliability (short interval) before interpreting long-interval change.
  • Report rank-order, mean-level, and profile stability separately.
  • Model change by age band, expecting genuinely lower stability across 8–14.

One operational fact

The publication pathway depends on one thing: item-level, timestamped response data must be collected from day one. Research consent and follow-up items are embedded in the post-purchase flow at launch, so the dataset for a validation-and-stability paper accumulates as a by-product of ordinary sales.

13 — Ethics, framing & data separation

A duty of care

A personality product aimed at children carries responsibilities a career quiz does not. Adult and youth data are physically separated and never aggregated in any view or analysis.

  • Normal-range only. Axis 5 measures temperament, not pathology. The words neurotic, unstable, anxious never appear. The strengths-based frame is Sensitive ↔ Steady (cf. Aron's highly-sensitive-child work) — accurate, not spin.
  • The shadow is a growth edge, never a deficit — especially for a child. No profile tells a parent their child has a "dark side"; it shows where support helps (SDT competence).
  • Empower the parent, do not diagnose the child. Guidance is "how to support a child like this," never a verdict.
  • Transparency. The youth report states it is based on parent observation; the methodology is available to the high-involvement parent (ELM).
  • Consent for research is explicit, separate, and revocable — opt-in, never a condition of purchase.
